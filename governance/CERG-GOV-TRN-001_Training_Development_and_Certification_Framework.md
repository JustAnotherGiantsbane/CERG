38|38|## 1. Purpose and Scope
39|39|
40|40|Cybersecurity skills have a half-life measured in months, not years. A Detection Engineer whose knowledge stops advancing writes rules for last year's threats. A Cloud Security Engineer who does not track platform evolution reviews architectures against a mental model that is already obsolete. A NERC-CIP Compliance Manager who does not monitor regulatory developments discovers a new requirement when the auditor cites it.
41|41|
42|42|The Job Descriptions (JD-001) list relevant certifications per role. They do not define which are required, which are recommended, who pays, or how certification connects to progression. The Competency Model (CMP-001) defines what good looks like at each grade. It does not define the path to get there. This document closes both gaps.
43|43|
44|44|It provides a role-to-certification matrix with three tiers (required, recommended, aspirational) per role and grade, a training curriculum mapped to CMP-001 competency domains, a cross-training standard that operationalizes the left-right knowledge model into a measurable time commitment, a professional development budget guideline, and conference and external engagement expectations.
45|45|
46|46|It applies to every CERG team member. Certifications are role-dependent; the training philosophy, cross-training expectation, and budget guidelines are universal.
47|47|
48|48|> **Training Is an Investment, Not a Perk**
49|49|>
50|50|> An organization that treats training as a reward for good performance is sending a signal that capability development is optional. An organization that treats training as a recurring operational expense, budgeted, tracked, and expected, is building a team that gets better every quarter. The first organization loses its best people to the second.

54|54|## 2. Training Philosophy
55|55|
56|56|1. **Development is a shared responsibility.** The organization provides the budget, the time, and the curriculum. The individual provides the effort, the curiosity, and the follow-through. Neither party can succeed without the other.
57|57|
58|58|2. **Certifications validate, they do not replace.** A certification confirms that a person has demonstrated a body of knowledge at a point in time. It does not confirm that they apply it effectively in their role. Certifications are useful for hiring credibility, regulatory compliance, and structured learning. They are not a substitute for demonstrated competency.
59|59|
60|60|3. **Training closes the gap between current and target.** Every development activity should connect to a gap identified in the person's performance review (PERF-001) or their self-assessment against the competency model (CMP-001). "I want to learn Kubernetes security" is a valid interest. "I need to learn Kubernetes security because my next-grade competency anchor requires architecture reviews of containerized workloads" is a development plan.
61|61|
62|62|4. **Cross-training is not optional.** The left-right knowledge model (FRM-001 §9.2) depends on every CERG team member understanding the other two pillars well enough to collaborate effectively. Cross-training is a standing expectation, not a discretionary activity.
63|63|
64|64|5. **Knowledge shared is knowledge multiplied.** If the organization sends a person to a conference, a training course, or a certification boot camp, that person presents what they learned to the team. The organization's return on the training investment includes not just one person's capability gain but the team's.

69|69|
70|70|### 3.1 Certification Tiers
71|71|
72|72|| Tier | Definition | Who Pays | Timeline |
73|73||---|---|---|---|
74|74|| **Required** | The person must hold this certification to perform the role. Required certifications are listed in the job description and verified at hire or within a defined post-hire window. | Organization | Maintained continuously; new hires have 6-12 months to obtain if not held at hire |
75|75|| **Recommended** | The certification materially improves the person's capability in their role and is expected within the first 18-24 months. | Organization | Expected within 18-24 months of role entry or grade advancement |

345|345|## 5. Cross-Training: The 5% Rule
346|346|
347|347|### 5.1 The Standard
348|348|
349|349|Every CERG team member is expected to spend at least 5% of their working time on cross-pillar learning and engagement. For a full-time team member, this is approximately:
350|350|
351|351|- **2 hours per week**, or
352|352|- **One day per month**, or
353|353|- **Two weeks per year**
354|354|
355|355|This is a performance expectation, not a suggestion. A person who has not engaged with the other two pillars in a quarter should be asked why during their quarterly check-in.
356|356|
357|357|### 5.2 What Counts
358|358|
359|359|Acceptable cross-training activities include:
360|360|
361|361|- Cross-pillar rotation days beyond the onboarding program (ONB-001 §9)
362|362|- Participating in cross-pillar working groups or projects
363|363|- Attending standing meetings of another pillar (as an observer-contributor)
364|364|- Shadowing a senior practitioner in another pillar for a specific activity
365|365|- Reading and providing feedback on a draft standard or procedure from another pillar
366|366|- Jointly investigating a cross-pillar incident or finding
367|367|- Teaching a brown-bag session on a topic relevant to another pillar
368|368|- Self-directed study of another pillar's tools, frameworks, or methods
369|369|
370|370|### 5.3 Tracking
371|371|
372|372|The OM-001 §10.4 cross-training log captures cross-training activities. Managers review the log during quarterly check-ins. The log does not need to be granular (it is not a timesheet). It needs to answer: in the last quarter, what did this person do to understand the other two pillars better?
373|373|
374|374|> **The 5% Rule Is a Minimum, Not a Cap**
375|375|>
376|376|> An Advisor who spends 10% of their time on cross-pillar work because they are leading a cross-pillar initiative is not violating the rule; they are demonstrating advanced Cross-Pillar Fluency. The 5% floor is for team members who are not naturally pulled into cross-pillar work through their role. It ensures that cross-pillar understanding does not become a privilege of seniority.

380|380|## 6. Professional Development Budget
381|381|
382|382|### 6.1 Per-Person Allocation
383|383|
384|384|CERG organizations should budget an annual per-person professional development allocation. The recommended baseline, adjusted for organizational size and budget, is:
385|385|
386|386|| Allocation Level | Amount (USD, Annual) | Appropriate For |
387|387||---|---|---|
388|388|| **Minimum** | $3,000 | Early-stage organization, constrained budget. Covers certification exam fees, one online course or local conference, and basic training platform access. |
389|389|| **Target** | $5,000-$8,000 | Established program. Covers certification training and exam, one major conference or SANS course, and ongoing training platform access. |
390|390|| **Competitive** | $10,000+ | Mature program competing for top-tier talent. Covers SANS training + GIAC certification, major industry conference attendance, executive education, and external coaching for managers. |
391|391|
392|392|### 6.2 What the Budget Covers
393|393|
394|394|The professional development budget covers:
395|395|
396|396|| Expense | Covered? | Notes |
397|397||---|---|---|
398|398|| Certification exam fees | Yes | First attempt only; retakes require manager approval and a study plan |
399|399|| Certification preparation courses or materials | Yes | Up to reasonable cost; self-study materials are preferred over boot camps unless the certification requires hands-on lab access |
400|400|| Conference registration | Yes | With conference attendance criteria (§7) |
401|401|| Conference travel and accommodation | Yes | Per organizational travel policy |
402|402|| Training platform subscriptions | Yes | Organization-wide licenses where cost-effective |
403|403|| Academic tuition (degree programs) | Partial or case-by-case | Tuition reimbursement per organizational policy; must be relevant to the person's role or progression |
404|404|| Professional association memberships | Yes | ISACA, ISC2, ISSA, SANS, ISAC memberships |
405|405|| Books and self-study materials | Yes | Reasonable cost; manager approval for items over a defined threshold |
406|406|
407|407|### 6.3 Budget Administration
408|408|
409|409|- The budget is per person, not per team. A manager cannot reallocate a departing team member's unspent budget to a conference trip for themselves.
410|410|- Unspent budget does not roll over. The annual cycle encourages regular development activity, not end-of-year spending sprees.
411|411|- Budget requests beyond the annual allocation require a business case: what will the person learn, how does it connect to their development plan, and what is the expected organizational benefit?

415|415|## 7. Conference and External Engagement
416|416|
417|417|### 7.1 Conference Attendance Criteria
418|418|
419|419|The organization supports conference attendance when:
420|420|
421|421|1. The conference content is directly relevant to the person's role or development priorities.
422|422|2. The person commits to presenting what they learned to the team within two weeks of returning (a 30-minute brown-bag session, a written summary, or an internal training module).
423|423|3. Conference attendance is distributed fairly across the team. The same person attending three conferences while a peer attends none is a signal that the team's development investment is not equitable.
424|424|
425|425|### 7.2 Priority Conferences by Role Family
426|426|
427|427|| Role Family | Priority Conferences |
428|428||---|---|
429|429|| **Engineering** | AWS re:Inforce, Microsoft Ignite (security track), Google Cloud Next (security track), KubeCon (security track), fwd:cloudsec, SANS Security East/West |
430|430|| **Risk** | RSA Conference, Black Hat, DEF CON, SANS Threat Hunting & IR Summit, FIRST Conference, sector-specific ISAC summits |
431|431|| **Governance** | RSA Conference (GRC track), ISACA conferences, IIA conferences, NIST workshops, sector-specific regulatory conferences |
432|432|| **Cross-Cutting** | Local BSides events, OWASP Global AppSec, regional cybersecurity summits |
433|433|
434|434|### 7.3 External Engagement Expectations
435|435|
436|436|At the Advisor (S3) level and above, external engagement is an expectation, not a perk:
437|437|
438|438|| Grade | External Engagement Expectation |
439|439||---|---|
440|440|| **S1-S2** | Encouraged to attend local events and webinars. Conference attendance supported when aligned to development priorities. |
441|441|| **S3** | Expected to attend at least one major conference per year. Encouraged to submit a presentation proposal. Participating in an industry working group or ISAC. |
442|442|| **S4** | Expected to present at conferences, participate in industry working groups or standards bodies, and contribute to the broader cybersecurity community. External engagement is part of the Sr. Advisor's influence dimension (CMP-001 §4.6). |
443|443|
444|444|> **External Engagement Is Recruitment**
445|445|>
446|446|> A Sr. Advisor who presents at RSA, contributes to a NIST working group, and is known in their sector's threat-sharing community is not just developing themselves. They are making the organization visible as a place where serious cybersecurity practitioners work. A candidate who researches the organization and finds its engineers presenting at conferences is more likely to apply than one who finds no public presence at all. External engagement is part of the employer brand strategy.

452|452|### 8.1 Individual Development Plan
453|453|
454|454|Each team member maintains an Individual Development Plan (IDP) as part of their performance record (PERF-001 §9). The IDP is updated semi-annually and contains:
455|455|

457|457|- Specific training activities, certifications, or experiences planned to address each priority
458|458|- Timeline and estimated cost
459|459|- Status tracking (planned, in progress, completed)
460|460|
461|461|### 8.2 Team Training Calendar
462|462|
463|463|Each pillar leader maintains a team training calendar that:
464|464|
465|465|- Tracks certification expirations and renewal deadlines
466|466|- Schedules internal knowledge-sharing sessions (brown-bag presentations, tool training, cross-pillar briefings)
467|467|- Coordinates conference attendance to avoid staffing gaps
468|468|- Identifies team-wide training needs (e.g., a new regulatory framework everyone needs to understand)
469|469|
470|470|### 8.3 Training Record
471|471|
472|472|The organization maintains a training record for each team member as part of their HR file. The record documents:
473|473|
474|474|- Certifications held, with issue and expiration dates
475|475|- Formal training courses completed
476|476|- Conference attendance
477|477|- Internal training delivered (the person teaching counts as development activity)
478|478|
479|479|The record supports regulatory compliance (NERC-CIP CIP-004 requires documented training for personnel with access to BES Cyber Systems; CMMC requires documented role-based training) and provides the evidence base for promotion cases that reference certification achievements.
